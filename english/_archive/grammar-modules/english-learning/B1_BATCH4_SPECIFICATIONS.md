## Module 16: Defining vs Non-Defining Relative Clauses

**Full Title:** Defining vs Non-Defining Relative Clauses
**Subtitle:** Essential vs Extra Information - Comma Usage
**Grammar Focus:** Distinguishing between essential and additional information
**Estimated Time:** 6-7 hours
**Exercise Count:** 95 (high - critical B1 feature)
**Difficulty:** ★★★★☆ (Challenging - requires understanding of information essentiality)

### Core Concept

Two types of relative clauses with different purposes and punctuation:

**Defining Relative Clauses:**
- Provide ESSENTIAL information to identify the noun
- No commas
- Cannot be removed without changing meaning
- Can use "that"

**Non-Defining Relative Clauses:**
- Provide EXTRA information (bonus details)
- Commas required
- Can be removed without changing essential meaning
- CANNOT use "that" - use who, which, where, when

### Defining Relative Clauses (Review from A2)

**Structure:** No commas

**Examples:**
- "The woman **who works at the bank** is my neighbor."
  - Which woman? The one who works at the bank (essential to identify)
- "The book **that I'm reading** is very interesting."
  - Which book? The one I'm reading (essential)
- "The restaurant **where we had dinner** was excellent."
  - Which restaurant? Where we had dinner (essential)

**Key:** The relative clause is NECESSARY to know which person/thing we mean.

### Non-Defining Relative Clauses (NEW at B1)

**Structure:** Commas before and after (or before if at end)

**Examples:**
- "My mother, **who is 65**, lives in London."
  - I have only one mother - "who is 65" is extra information
  - Commas show it's additional, not essential
- "Paris, **which is the capital of France**, is a beautiful city."
  - Everyone knows Paris - the clause adds bonus information
- "We visited the Eiffel Tower, **which was built in 1889**."
  - We already know which building - this is extra historical detail

**Key:** The relative clause adds EXTRA information, but we already know which person/thing.

### The Comma Rule - Critical!

**Defining (NO commas):** Essential information to identify
- "The students **who studied hard** passed." (only those who studied)

**Non-Defining (WITH commas):** Extra information
- "The students, **who studied hard**, passed." (all students studied and all passed)

**Meaning changes with commas!**

### That vs Which

**Defining clauses:**
- Can use "that" OR "which"
- "that" more common in speech
- "The car **that/which** I bought is red."

**Non-defining clauses:**
- MUST use "which" (NOT "that")
- "My car, **which** is red, needs repairs." ✅
- "My car, **that** is red, needs repairs." ❌

### Who, Which, Where, When in Non-Defining

- **Who** (people): "My teacher, **who** is very experienced, helped me."
- **Which** (things): "The book, **which** cost $20, was worth it."
- **Where** (places): "London, **where** I was born, has changed a lot."
- **When** (time): "Summer, **when** it's hot, is my favorite season."

## Module 17: Relative Clauses - Whose and Whom

**Full Title:** Relative Clauses with Whose and Whom
**Subtitle:** Possession and Formal Object Relatives
**Grammar Focus:** Advanced relative pronouns
**Estimated Time:** 5-6 hours
**Exercise Count:** 90
**Difficulty:** ★★★☆☆ (Moderate - vocabulary-focused)

### Core Concept

Two special relative pronouns:

**Whose:** Shows possession (replaces his/her/their/its)
- "The woman **whose** car is red lives next door."
- = The woman's car is red. She lives next door.

**Whom:** Formal object of relative clause (replaces him/her/them)
- "The person **to whom** I spoke was helpful."
- = I spoke to him/her. The person was helpful.

### Whose for Possession

**Formation:** whose + noun (the possessed thing)

**Examples:**
- "The student **whose** bag was stolen reported it."
  - Student's bag (possession)
- "That's the house **whose** roof is damaged."
  - House's roof (possession)
- "I know a woman **whose** son is a doctor."
  - Woman's son (possession)

**Key:** Whose replaces possessive (his/her/their/its) + noun

**Works with people AND things:**
- People: "The man whose car broke down..."
- Things: "The country whose economy is growing..."

### Whose vs Who's

**Common confusion!**

- **Whose** = possession (relative pronoun)
  - "The man whose car is red..."
- **Who's** = who is / who has (contraction)
  - "Who's coming?" = "Who is coming?"

**Never confuse these!**

### Whom - Formal Object

**When to use:** Formal English, especially with prepositions

**Formation:** (preposition +) whom

**Examples:**
- "The person **to whom** I spoke was helpful."
  - (I spoke to him/her)
- "The colleague **with whom** I work is experienced."
  - (I work with him/her)
- "The professor **from whom** I learned a lot retired."
  - (I learned from him/her)

**Informal alternatives:**
- Formal: "The person **to whom** I spoke..."
- Informal: "The person **(who/that)** I spoke **to**..."
  - (Preposition moves to end, whom → who/that)

### Whom vs Who

**Object position:**
- Formal: whom
- Informal: who (or that)
- Very informal: can omit

**Examples:**
- Formal: "The man **whom** I met was kind."
- Informal: "The man **who** I met was kind."
- Very informal: "The man I met was kind." (omitted)

**With preposition at start:**
- Must use "whom"
- "The person **to whom** I gave the book..."
- ❌ "The person to who I gave the book..."

**With preposition at end:**
- Can use "who" or "that" or omit
- "The person **(who/that)** I gave the book **to**..."

## Module 18: Relative Clauses - Advanced Patterns

**Full Title:** Relative Clauses - Object Relatives and Omission
**Subtitle:** When You Can Leave Out the Relative Pronoun
**Grammar Focus:** Object relatives, omission, and reduction
**Estimated Time:** 5-6 hours
**Exercise Count:** 85
**Difficulty:** ★★★★☆ (Challenging - complex patterns)

### Core Concept

Advanced relative clause patterns:
1. **Object relatives:** Pronoun refers to object of relative clause
2. **Omission:** When relative pronoun can be omitted
3. **Reduced relatives:** Shortened clauses (advanced)

### Subject vs Object Relatives

**Subject Relative:** Pronoun is subject of relative clause
- "The man **who lives** next door is friendly."
- Who = subject of "lives"
- CANNOT omit "who"

**Object Relative:** Pronoun is object of relative clause
- "The man **(who/that)** I met yesterday was friendly."
- Who/that = object of "met" (I met him)
- CAN omit "who/that"

**Key test:** If relative pronoun is followed immediately by a verb, it's usually subject (can't omit). If followed by subject + verb, it's object (can omit).

### Omitting Relative Pronouns

**Can omit when:**
- Pronoun is object of relative clause
- In defining clauses only
- Informal/conversational English

**Examples:**
- Full: "The book **that** I bought is interesting."
- Omitted: "The book I bought is interesting." ✅

**Cannot omit when:**
- Pronoun is subject
- In non-defining clauses
- ❌ "My mother, is a teacher, lives in London." (need "who")

### Common Omissions

**With verbs:**
- "The movie (that) I saw was great."
- "The person (who/that) I met was nice."
- "The car (that) he bought is expensive."

**With prepositions:**
- "The person (who/that) I spoke to was helpful."
- "The book (that) I told you about is here."

### Reduced Relative Clauses (Advanced)

**Present participle (-ing):**
- Full: "The man **who is standing** by the door is my boss."
- Reduced: "The man **standing** by the door is my boss."

**Past participle (-ed):**
- Full: "The car **which was stolen** was found."
- Reduced: "The car **stolen** was found."

**Note:** This is advanced B1/B2 - introduced here, mastered later

## Module 19: Modal Verbs for Deduction - Present

**Full Title:** Modal Verbs for Deduction - Must, Can't, May, Might, Could
**Subtitle:** Making Logical Conclusions About the Present
**Grammar Focus:** Using modals for inference and deduction
**Estimated Time:** 5-6 hours
**Exercise Count:** 90
**Difficulty:** ★★★★☆ (Challenging - new modal function)

### Core Concept

Using modal verbs to make DEDUCTIONS (logical conclusions) based on evidence:

**Certainty Scale:**
- **100% certain positive:** must be
- **100% certain negative:** can't be / couldn't be
- **50% possible:** may be / might be / could be

**Different from A2 modals:**
- A2: must = obligation ("You must study")
- B1: must = deduction ("He must be tired" - I'm certain based on evidence)

### Must (Be) - Certainty (Positive)

**Meaning:** I'm certain this is true (based on evidence)

**Formation:** must + be/verb

**Examples:**
- "He's yawning. He **must be** tired." (evidence → certain conclusion)
- "The lights are on. Someone **must be** home." (evidence → deduction)
- "She speaks perfect English. She **must be** British." (evidence → conclusion)

**Key:** Based on evidence, not rules/obligations!

### Can't (Be) / Couldn't (Be) - Impossibility

**Meaning:** I'm certain this is NOT true (impossible based on evidence)

**Formation:** can't be / couldn't be

**Examples:**
- "He's just eaten. He **can't be** hungry." (impossible)
- "She's only 30. She **can't be** your grandmother." (impossible)
- "That **can't be** right. Let me check." (certain it's wrong)

**Note:** "Couldn't be" slightly more polite/tentative than "can't be"

### May (Be) / Might (Be) / Could (Be) - Possibility

**Meaning:** It's possible (about 50% - not certain)

**Formation:** may be / might be / could be

**Examples:**
- "He **might be** at home. I'm not sure." (possible)
- "She **may be** busy. Try calling later." (possible)
- "That **could be** the answer." (possible)

**All three mean roughly the same:** about 50% possibility

**Slight differences:**
- May: neutral
- Might: slightly less certain than may
- Could: suggests considering possibility

## Module 20: Modal Verbs for Deduction - Past

**Full Title:** Modal Verbs for Past Deduction - Must Have, Can't Have, Might Have
**Subtitle:** Making Conclusions About Past Events
**Grammar Focus:** Past modal deduction with perfect forms
**Estimated Time:** 6-7 hours
**Exercise Count:** 95 (high - complex structure)
**Difficulty:** ★★★★★ (Very challenging - requires perfect aspect understanding)

### Core Concept

Using modals to make deductions about the PAST:

**Formation:** modal + have + past participle

**Certainty Scale (Past):**
- **100% certain positive:** must have + pp
- **100% certain negative:** can't have + pp / couldn't have + pp
- **50% possible:** may have + pp / might have + pp / could have + pp

**Prerequisite:** Must understand past perfect from Batch 1!

### Must Have + Past Participle - Certainty (Past)

**Meaning:** I'm certain this happened (based on evidence)

**Formation:** must have + past participle

**Examples:**
- "The ground is wet. It **must have rained**." (certain conclusion)
- "She looks upset. Someone **must have told** her the news." (certain)
- "He wasn't at work. He **must have been** sick." (logical conclusion)

### Can't Have / Couldn't Have + PP - Impossibility (Past)

**Meaning:** I'm certain this didn't happen (impossible)

**Formation:** can't have + pp / couldn't have + pp

**Examples:**
- "He **can't have taken** the bus. It wasn't running." (impossible)
- "She **couldn't have known**. Nobody told her." (impossible)
- "They **can't have finished** already. It's too soon." (impossible)

### May Have / Might Have / Could Have + PP - Possibility (Past)

**Meaning:** It's possible this happened (not certain)

**Formation:** may have + pp / might have + pp / could have + pp

**Examples:**
- "He **might have gone** home already." (possible)
- "She **may have forgotten** about the meeting." (possible)
- "They **could have missed** the train." (possible)

### Should Have / Ought to Have - Expectation Not Met

**Different meaning:** Expected to happen but didn't

**Examples:**
- "He **should have arrived** by now." (expected but hasn't)
- "They **should have called**." (expected behavior)

**Note:** This is criticism/expectation, not deduction!
