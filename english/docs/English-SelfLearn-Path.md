This is a comprehensive 12-18 month self-learning program using free AI tools to advance from B1 to C1 English, with emphasis on professional communication. With 12 hours weekly, you can realistically reach B2 in 5-7 months and C1 within 12-18 months total.

## Understanding your progression: B1 → B2 → C1

**At B1 (your starting point):** You can handle familiar work and travel situations, understand clear speech on familiar topics, and write simple connected texts. You have about 2,000-2,500 active words and can express basic opinions. Your challenge is spontaneity—you need preparation time and often search for words.

**At B2 (your first major goal):** You'll interact with native speakers fluently without strain, understand complex texts including technical material in your field, and write clear detailed texts on diverse subjects. Your vocabulary expands to 3,000-4,000 words including specialized professional terms. You can lead meetings, present to stakeholders, and handle complex negotiations. This is the threshold for most professional environments.

**At C1 (advanced proficiency):** You'll express ideas fluently without obvious searching for words, understand demanding texts with implicit meanings, and use language flexibly for social, academic, and professional purposes. With 4,000-8,000 active words including idiomatic expressions, you'll understand native speaker jokes, watch films without subtitles, and think naturally in English.

## Your 12-hour weekly schedule

This schedule balances all skills while emphasizing professional communication. Each activity integrates AI tools for maximum effectiveness.

### Daily foundation (Monday-Friday, 1.5 hours)

**Morning routine (20 minutes)**
- **Vocabulary building (10 min):** Use WordUp app or Memrise with AI integration. Learn 20-30 new words weekly, focusing on business contexts. WordUp's Knowledge Map identifies exactly which words you know and prioritizes the most useful words from 25,000 ranked by importance.
- **Quick reading (10 min):** Read one article from Breaking News English at your level or News in Levels. Save new vocabulary for AI practice later.

**Lunch break (15 minutes)**
- **Podcast listening:** Choose from British Council LearnEnglish Podcasts (A2-B1), Practising English (B1-B2), or Real Exam English (B2+). Listen with transcripts initially, then without. The Real Exam English podcast is exceptional because it features native speakers from UK, USA, Ireland, Australia, New Zealand, and South Africa answering real exam questions.

**Evening core practice (45-60 minutes)**
- **AI conversation (15 min):** Use Gliglish (10 free minutes daily) or LangAI for structured speaking practice. These tools provide real-time grammar corrections during conversations, pronunciation feedback, and role-play scenarios.
- **Grammar or writing practice (30 min):** Alternate days between grammar study with ALULA's 39+ courses and writing practice with Grammarly/Khan Academy Writing Coach for feedback.
- **Review and consolidation (15 min):** Discuss your daily learning with ChatGPT or Claude, practice new vocabulary in context, or review AI corrections from earlier exercises.

### Weekend intensive sessions (Saturday and Sunday, 2.5 hours each)

**Saturday: Speaking and listening immersion**
- Watch one TED Talk or BBC Learning English video with subtitles (20 min)
- Note 10-15 key phrases and expressions (10 min)
- Practice presentation on same topic with AI, get feedback (30 min)
- Listen to longer podcast episode, discuss with AI using new vocabulary (40 min)
- Role-play workplace scenarios with AI: job interviews, client meetings, presentations (50 min)

**Sunday: Writing and comprehensive review**
- Write professional emails or reports (40 min), get AI feedback with corrections explained
- Read longer article or short story (30 min), discuss themes and vocabulary with AI
- Weekly progress review: test yourself with AI-generated quizzes on week's content (30 min)
- Plan next week's learning priorities based on identified weak areas (20 min)
- Free practice on personal interests in English—hobby research, YouTube videos, casual AI conversations (30 min)

```

```

## Your learning path by level

### Phase 1: Consolidating B1 (Weeks 1-8)

**Priority focus:** Build speaking confidence and solidify grammar foundations. At this stage, fluency matters more than perfect accuracy—you need to overcome hesitation and develop repair strategies.

**Grammar targets:** Master all basic tenses (present, past, future, present perfect), subordinate clauses with "because," "although," "when," and basic conditional sentences (if + present, will + verb). Use ALULA's 39+ grammar courses systematically.

**Vocabulary goal:** Expand from 2,000 to 2,500 active words, focusing on the most common business terms. WordUp teaches the 1,000 most common words systematically—prioritize these first.

**Speaking practice:** Daily 10-minute Gliglish sessions focusing on workplace scenarios like phone calls, giving updates, asking for clarification, and making polite requests. Start with prepared responses, gradually moving toward spontaneous conversation.

**Listening development:** Train your ear for natural speed with British Council podcasts and 6 Minute English. Listen to each episode twice: once with transcript, once without. Use ALULA's "Hard Mode" (no text shown) to challenge yourself.

**Writing basics:** Practice writing 100-150 word emails daily using Grammarly for instant corrections. Focus on clear structure: greeting, purpose, details, closing. Learn from every correction explanation.

**Milestone assessment (Week 8):** Take the EF SET test for your baseline score. You should be able to hold a 15-minute conversation on familiar topics, understand 70-80% of clear standard speech, and write coherent paragraphs with few major errors.

### Phase 2: Advancing to B2 (Months 3-7)

**Priority focus:** Refine accuracy while maintaining fluency. Eliminate persistent grammar errors and expand vocabulary breadth. Develop the ability to argue positions and explain complex ideas.

**Grammar advancement:** Master passive voice (especially in business contexts like "The proposal was approved"), reported speech ("She said that..."), advanced conditionals (second and third type), and complex sentence structures with multiple clauses. Practice these specifically with AI: "Create 10 business scenarios where I need to use passive voice, then let me practice."

**Vocabulary expansion:** Add 1,500-2,000 specialized words including phrasal verbs (follow up, bring up, put off), business collocations (reach an agreement, meet a deadline, launch a campaign), and formal expressions. Use AI prompt: "Provide 10 business English phrasal verbs related to project management with definitions and example sentences."

**Speaking sophistication:** Move beyond simple transactions to debates and structured arguments. Practice with AI: "Let's debate whether remote work is better than office work. Challenge my arguments and help me defend my position using better vocabulary." Focus on discourse markers (however, moreover, on the other hand) for coherent speech.

**Advanced listening:** Progress to Real Exam English podcast (B2 level) and BBC News Review. Watch TED Talks at normal speed with English subtitles initially, then without. Bob the Canadian's YouTube videos show authentic real-life situations in hardware stores, hotels, and hospitals.

**Professional writing:** Write longer texts (300-500 words) including project updates, proposals, and structured reports using Khan Academy Writing Coach. Practice formal vs. informal register: "Rephrase this sentence in 3 ways: more formal (for reports), more casual (for team communication), more concise (for executive summary)."

**Common B2 challenges:** You'll struggle with articles (a/an/the), prepositions (in/on/at for time and place), and uncountable nouns. Address these systematically with AI: "Check this text ONLY for article errors and explain each mistake." The most critical errors to eliminate: subject-verb agreement ("The team are working" → "The team is working"), incorrect verb patterns ("I recommend you to try" → "I recommend trying"), and tense confusion between present perfect and past simple.

**Milestone assessment (Month 7):** Retake EF SET—you should score solidly in B2 range. You should participate comfortably in work meetings, understand 80-90% of standard speech, watch some TV content without subtitles, and write multi-paragraph professional documents with good structure. Most importantly, you should interact with native speakers without strain for both parties.

### Phase 3: Reaching C1 (Months 8-18)

**Priority focus:** Achieve natural fluency, master idiomatic expressions, and develop register flexibility. The goal is to sound natural rather than textbook-correct, understand implicit meanings and humor, and adapt seamlessly to any professional context.

**Grammar refinement:** Perfect nuanced grammar including subjunctive mood, advanced conditionals (mixed types), reported speech with modal verbs, and sophisticated connector usage. Focus on eliminating any remaining systematic errors through AI pattern analysis.

**Vocabulary mastery:** Expand to 6,000-8,000 active words including extensive idiomatic expressions, cultural references, and field-specific terminology. Learn synonyms for precise expression: "Give me 10 alternatives to 'important' with subtle differences in meaning and usage contexts."

**Speaking naturalness:** Practice thinking in English without translating. Engage AI in abstract discussions: "Let's discuss the implications of AI on employment. Challenge my reasoning and push me to explain complex ideas." Focus on eliminating hesitation, developing natural rhythm and intonation, and mastering subtle humor.

**Advanced listening:** Consume native-level content exclusively—podcasts at normal speed (practice with various accents using Real Exam English), films without subtitles, and complex lectures. Lucy's British English YouTube channel covers idioms and slang essential for sounding natural.

**Sophisticated writing:** Produce professional reports, proposals, and academic-style essays with varied sentence structures and stylistic elegance. Practice with AI: "Review this proposal for a client. Suggest ways to make it more persuasive while maintaining professionalism." Develop your ability to write with appropriate tone for different audiences.

**Cultural fluency:** Understand references, expressions, and implicit meanings that natives take for granted. Ask AI: "Explain common British business idioms and their origins" or "What are typical communication styles in American corporate culture?"

**Milestone assessment (Month 12 and 18):** Take comprehensive assessments quarterly. At C1, you should think in English naturally, rarely need subtitles for standard content, understand 90%+ of native conversations, discuss abstract topics spontaneously, catch cultural references, and write with stylistic variety. The ultimate test: can you successfully tell a joke in English and have natives laugh?

## Common mistakes you'll make (and how AI fixes them)

### Critical grammar errors at B1-B2 level

**Preposition confusion:** You'll frequently write "I will meet you in Monday" (correct: "on Monday"), "I live in [specific address]" (correct: "at [address]"), "I'm waiting the results" (correct: "waiting for"). Use AI prompt: "Check this text ONLY for preposition errors and explain each mistake: [TEXT]"

**Article nightmares:** The distinction between a/an/the remains challenging. Common errors: "A hour" (correct: "An hour" - based on sound), "The happiness is important" (correct: "Happiness is important" - no article for abstract concepts). Few vs. a few changes meaning completely: "few people" means almost none, "a few people" means some.

**Tense confusion:** Present perfect vs. past simple trips up most learners. "Last summer, I have visited London" is wrong—use past simple with specific past time. "Did you see my keys?" should be "Have you seen my keys?" when timing doesn't matter. AI correction prompt: "I often confuse present perfect and past simple. Review this text focusing on tense accuracy and explain each correction."

**Verb patterns:** You'll incorrectly write "I recommend you to try" (correct: "I recommend trying"), "Do you mind to help" (correct: "Do you mind helping"), "I look forward to see you" (correct: "to seeing you"). These must be memorized through repeated AI practice.

### Vocabulary pitfalls

**Uncountable nouns:** Advice, information, furniture, baggage, equipment, knowledge never take plural. "She gave me good advices" is wrong. Correct forms: "good advice" or "a piece of advice." Create AI flashcards: "Generate 20 practice sentences where I choose between countable and uncountable forms."

**False friends:** "Actually" means "in fact," not "currently." "Assist" means "help," not "attend." "Sensible" means "practical," not "sensitive." "According to" requires third-party sources, never "according to me" (use "in my opinion"). Build awareness with AI: "What are the most common false friends for [your native language] speakers learning English?"

**Professional writing errors:** Never use contractions (I'm, don't, can't) in formal documents. Avoid starting sentences with "And" or "But" in business writing. Eliminate redundant subjects: "My handwriting it is improving" → "My handwriting is improving." Use AI tone-checker: "Review this email for formality and suggest professional improvements."

**Step 1: Establish your baseline (Week 1):** Write 300-500 words on a familiar topic. Use comprehensive AI correction: "Assume the role of an EFL teacher. Highlight any grammar or spelling mistakes, insert them into a table with three columns (Error | Type of Error | Correction), provide suggestions for overall improvement, point out strong points, and suggest more advanced vocabulary. [PASTE TEXT]"

**Step 2: Identify patterns (Week 2):** Request pattern analysis: "I've been making these mistakes in my writing [paste several corrected examples]. Analyze these errors and identify patterns in the types of mistakes I make most frequently. Categorize them and explain why I might be making these errors. Create a personalized checklist I can use before submitting writing."

**Step 3: Targeted improvement (Weeks 3-8):** Focus on one error category at a time. Week 3-4: "Check this text ONLY for preposition errors." Week 5-6: "Check ONLY for article usage." This focused approach prevents overwhelm and creates lasting improvement.

**Step 4: Progressive complexity (Ongoing):** As you improve, request higher-level feedback: "Analyze my word choice. Highlight instances where I used a word incorrectly, used less natural expressions when better ones exist, or made collocation errors. Suggest professional alternatives."

**Step 5: Regular assessment:** Monthly progress check: "Here's my writing from this month. Compare it to last month's writing. Create a progress report showing which error types have decreased, which persist, new errors that appeared, and specific recommendations for next month."

**Monthly can-do checklist:** Rate yourself 1-10 on these statements:
- I can organize thoughts clearly when speaking
- I feel confident speaking with native speakers
- I can understand native speakers at normal speed
- I use varied vocabulary (not repeating same words)
- I can write professional emails/messages/texts
- I can participate in meetings/discussions
- I understand humor and implicit meanings
- I can read authentic texts (news, articles, books)

**Content revisit method:** Select a movie requiring subtitles initially. After 6 months learning, rewatch without subtitles and track comprehension improvement percentage. Apply same method to podcasts and articles.

**Weekly learning journal:** Document in English:
- What you learned this week
- Mistakes you made and corrections
- New vocabulary acquired
- Conversations you had (with AI or humans)
- Challenges you faced
- Improvements you noticed

Have AI review your journal for error patterns and progress indicators.
