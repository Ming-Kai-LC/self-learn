# Additional Resources for Data Science Research

This document provides curated resources to deepen your research skills beyond this course.

## Research Databases

### General Academic Databases
- **Google Scholar**: https://scholar.google.com
  - Free, comprehensive, covers all disciplines
  - Good starting point for any research

- **Semantic Scholar**: https://www.semanticscholar.org
  - AI-powered search and recommendations
  - Shows paper influence and connections

### Computer Science & Data Science
- **arXiv**: https://arxiv.org
  - Preprints in CS, Math, Stats, ML/AI
  - Cutting-edge research before peer review

- **ACM Digital Library**: https://dl.acm.org
  - Computer science and IT
  - Requires subscription (often through universities)

- **IEEE Xplore**: https://ieeexplore.ieee.org
  - Engineering and computer science
  - Requires subscription

### Domain-Specific
- **PubMed**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov
  - Biomedical and life sciences
  - Free access

- **SSRN**: https://www.ssrn.com
  - Social sciences
  - Economics, finance, business

- **bioRxiv**: https://www.biorxiv.org
  - Biology preprints
  - Free access

## Reference Management Tools

### Recommended: Zotero
- **Website**: https://www.zotero.org
- **Cost**: Free and open source
- **Features**: Browser integration, PDF annotation, citation generation
- **Best for**: Beginners and those who want free, reliable tools

### Alternative: Mendeley
- **Website**: https://www.mendeley.com
- **Cost**: Free (owned by Elsevier)
- **Features**: Similar to Zotero, plus social features
- **Best for**: Collaborative research

### Other Options
- **EndNote**: Paid, powerful, used in academia
- **Papers**: Mac-only, paid, beautiful interface
- **JabRef**: Free, BibTeX-focused

## Books on Research Methods

### Essential Reading
1. **"The Craft of Research"** by Wayne C. Booth, Gregory G. Colomb, Joseph M. Williams
   - The definitive guide to research
   - Covers question formulation to writing

2. **"How to Read a Paper"** by S. Keshav
   - The 3-pass method
   - Efficient academic reading

3. **"Research Design: Qualitative, Quantitative, and Mixed Methods Approaches"** by John W. Creswell
   - Comprehensive methodology guide
   - Good for planning studies

### Data Science Specific
4. **"The Practice of Reproducible Research"** (Free online)
   - Case studies in reproducible research
   - https://www.practicereproducibleresearch.org

5. **"Weapons of Math Destruction"** by Cathy O'Neil
   - Ethics and bias in algorithms
   - Real-world implications

6. **"Trustworthy Online Controlled Experiments"** by Kohavi, Tang, Xu
   - A/B testing at scale
   - Practical experimentation

## Conclusion

This is just a starting point! The field of data science research is vast and constantly evolving. The best way to stay current is to:

1. Follow researchers you admire on Twitter/LinkedIn
2. Subscribe to relevant newsletters
3. Attend conferences (virtual or in-person)
4. Join online communities
5. Read papers regularly
6. Practice your skills on real problems

**Remember**: The goal isn't to know everything, but to know where to find information when you need it.
